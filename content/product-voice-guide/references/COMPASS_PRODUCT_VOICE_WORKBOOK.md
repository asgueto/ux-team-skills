## Part 2: Define Your Registers

### Instructions

Every product has **at least 2, typically 3–4 registers**. A register is a type of user + their context.

Work with your team to identify who's talking to you, and what they need.

**Common registers:**
- **A** — Operator / Creator / Administrator (someone running the platform)
- **B** — User / Participant / Viewer (someone using it for a specific purpose)
- **C** — Mobile / Real-time context (someone in the moment, limited attention)
- **D** — Learner / Onboarding (someone new, building confidence)

Not all products need all four. GlobalLink Events has A, B, C. Compose has A, B, C. Some products just need A and B.

**Gender-neutral language:** In all register profiles, tone markers, and examples, use gender-neutral language. Refer to users as "they / their / them." Do not use "he / she" or "he or she." See Section 3 (Accessibility & inclusion) of the Compass Master Guide.

## Part 3: Position on Tone Spectrum

### Instructions

For each register, decide where on the **tone spectrum** your product sits.

The spectrum has four dimensions, each 1–10:
1. **Funny ↔ Serious** (default 6)
2. **Formal ↔ Casual** (default 5)
3. **Concise ↔ Detailed** (default 4)
4. **Respectful ↔ Irreverent** (default 3)

**When in doubt, use the default.** Only move if you have a good reason (high-stakes product, creative users, etc.).

**When Concise and Actionable are in tension:** Actionability beats brevity — if the most helpful next step needs more words, use them. Clarity beats concision — a shorter version that leaves users uncertain is not better. This is especially true in error states and high-stakes moments.

### Template

**Register A: [Name]**

- Funny (1–10): ☐ ☐ ☐ ☐ ☐ ☐ ☐ ☐ ☐ ☐ Serious
  Why this score? ________________

- Formal (1–10): ☐ ☐ ☐ ☐ ☐ ☐ ☐ ☐ Casual ☐
  Why this score? ________________

- Concise (1–10): ☐ ☐ Detailed ☐ ☐ ☐ ☐ ☐ ☐ ☐
  Why this score? ________________

- Respectful (1–10, inverted): ☐ (respectful) ☐ ☐ ☐ ☐ ☐ ☐ ☐ ☐ ☐ (irreverent)
  Why this score? ________________

*(Repeat for B, C, D)*

## Part 4: Define Product-Specific Terminology

### Instructions

List the key concepts in your product and the **exact term** you use for each, everywhere.

**Why this matters**: Consistency. If you say "session" in one place and "talk" in another, users get confused.

**Product naming:** In all documentation, handoff materials, and cross-product references, use the full name with the GlobalLink prefix: "GlobalLink Events," "GlobalLink Bookings." Within the product UI itself, the short name ("Events," "Bookings") is acceptable where context is already established. Never use "GL" as a prefix in client-facing content — always spell out "GlobalLink" in full. See Section 4 (Style & usage) of the Compass Master Guide.

## FAQs

**Q: Our product is too niche — can we skip some parts?**
A: No. Even niche products need clear registers, consistent terminology, and tone position. The structure is the same; only the details change.

**Q: Can we be funny in our product?**
A: Depends on stakes. High-stakes products (Portal, AI Interpreter) should avoid humor. Medium-stakes products (GlobalLink Events, GlobalLink Bookings) can use light moments in non-critical contexts. Low-stakes products can be slightly warmer.

**Q: What if our product spans multiple industries (e.g., we serve both legal AND retail)?**
A: Document both. Create registers for each industry, noting tone differences. Example: "Legal context: Formal (8/10). Retail context: Balanced (5/10)."

**Q: When does "Concise" conflict with "Actionable"?**
A: Often, especially in error messages. The resolution is a clear hierarchy: clarity beats concision — if being shorter leaves the user uncertain, shorter is wrong. Actionability beats brevity — if the most helpful next step requires an extra sentence, that sentence belongs there. Never cut a path forward for the sake of fewer words. This matters most in high-stakes moments: an error before a deadline should always include how to get help now, even if that makes the message longer.

**Q: When should we use "please" in copy?**
A: Avoid "please" in button labels and CTAs — buttons are actions, not requests. Avoid it in error messages — errors need clear instruction, not politeness. In instructional body text for warmer, lower-stakes flows, "please" can add warmth if it doesn't sound hollow. Default rule: write without "please" first, then add it only if removing it makes the message feel cold in context. See Section 4 (Style & usage) of the Compass Master Guide.

**Q: Should we wait for the perfect examples, or ship with what we have?**
A: Ship with 5 solid examples per register. You'll refine them quarterly. Perfect is enemy of done.

**Q: How often should we update this guide?**
